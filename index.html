Technology Evangelism

In my career as a technology evangelist I've given more than 500 presentations in more than 150 cities in 46 countries. There's nothing I enjoy more than being on stage in front of an audience somewhere in the world, sharing knowledge about some new technology or product.

Pro Tip #1: Do currency conversions before tipping, not after. I'm pretty sure that once I bought a waitress in Prague a new car.
I've given technical presentations, hands-on workshops, keynote addresses, and executive briefings, always with the goal of convincing the audience of three things:

  • Technology or product x is real, cutting-edge technology that is relevant right now
  • You can be productive with it today
  • If you're not using it, your competition probably is.

Over the years the target audiences for my work have been everything from developers and students to executives and analysts. I have worked with audiences in person at conferences and customer sites as well as remotely via webinars and podcasts. My presentations have won several awards, including a 2009 JavaOne Rock Star award for "Using SOAP and WS-* in the Cloud."

The technologies I have evangelized include cloud computing, REST, SOAP and the WS-* stack, XSLT, XML, Java, Eclipse, Service Component Architecture, and XForms. In all cases, I created the presentations or student handbooks that explained the technology and built the sample code or exercises that showed it in action. (As an example, you can take a look at the slides, the lab manual, and the sample code for the Service Component Architecture workshop I created.)

Pro Tip #2: When stuck on the streets of Mumbai at rush hour, your taxi driver may decide to cross the median of a 12-lane highway, drive at a high rate of speed into oncoming traffic, and then dodge back onto the correct side of the road at the last possible second. This is perfectly normal, legal, and unremarkable.
Since 2011 my evangelism efforts have been internally focused, with presentations, sample code, and research papers developed for IBM's sales staff, business partners, and select customers. My current assignment focuses on IBM's cloud portfolio, including the Bluemix PaaS and OpenStack-based products such as IBM Cloud Orchestrator. Driving revenue is my primary goal these days. In 2015 I helped close more than $31M in business.

Writing

I wrote XSLT, published by O'Reilly and Associates. Along with the text of the book, I created hundreds of sample stylesheets plus XSLT extension functions in Java, Perl, Python, Ruby, and C#. The book has sold 50,000 copies and counting and is also available in German and Russian translations.

Whatever the occasion, XSLT always makes a perfect gift for your friends and loved ones, as this unsolicited testimonial illustrates:

"When I gave [the book] to my girlfriend for her birthday, she just sat there in stunned silence. I can honestly say she hasn't looked at me the same way since. Thanks, XSLT Second Edition!"

—Email from a grateful reader, October 2014
I am a co-author of Programming Web Services with SOAP, also published by O'Reilly. That book has been translated into German and Italian.

During my time as the technical evangelist for IBM's developerWorks website, I wrote articles that have generated more than 4,000,000 page views over the years and were translated into Chinese, Japanese, Korean, Portuguese, Russian, and Vietnamese. My articles include:

  • An Introduction to XML (the most popular article for developerWorks' first 10 years)
  • The Simple Cloud API (a collaborative standards effort by Zend, IBM, Microsoft, Rackspace, and others)
  • Cloud Computing with PHP: Amazon S3, EC2, and SQS (no longer online)
  • Save Time and Code with XPath 2.0 and XSLT 2.0
  • HTML to Formatting Objects Conversion Guide

Development

I have more than a third of a century of development experience: I started by writing COBOL programs for a Jacquard loom early in Abraham Lincoln's second term. My code has run on everything from tablets to mobile phones to PDAs to desktops to VAXen to mainframes. I have written primarily in Java and JavaScript since the mid- to late-1990s, but have also coded for a living in PHP, C / C++, BASIC, and XSLT over the years.

In my Java experience I have written applets, servlets, an XML-based rendering engine for wizard-style help systems, XSLT extension functions to automate a web publishing workflow, web services, REST services, and EJBs.

My recent development projects have used node.js, AngularJS, NoSQL databases (primarily Couch and Mongo), pug (which I still call Jade), Bootstrap, and IBM's Bluemix Garage Method. I am currently focused on writing business rules and user interfaces for IBM products as well as single-page applications that showcase the Bluemix PaaS and the services it provides.

Pro Tip #3: According to a recent study by Forrester Research*, there are now roughly 7.3 billion JavaScript frameworks, more than one for every man, woman, and child on the face of the earth. When asked about a framework you've never heard of, don't admit to your lack of knowledge—simply sing the praises of a platform that doesn't exist. Example: "Aviato? Oh, I've built a few sites with it, but AngioplastyJS is way more scalable."

* Citation unavailable because I totally made this up.
As a side project, I am also building a mobile application using Ionic and Cordova to access a cloud-hosted NoSQL database.
